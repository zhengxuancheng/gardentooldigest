## What to Look For

### Wheel Type: Single vs. Double vs. Pneumatic vs. Flat-Free

The wheel is the most important component of a wheelbarrow. A bad wheel makes even a light load feel heavy.

**Pneumatic tires** (air-filled) roll smoothly over grass, gravel, and uneven ground. They absorb bumps and don't leave ruts in soft soil. The downside: flats. A thorn or nail leaves you stranded with a loaded barrow.

**Flat-free tires** (solid foam or hard rubber) never go flat. They're heavier, transfer more vibration to the handles, and can be harder to push on soft ground. For contractors and rough job sites, they're the standard.

**Single wheel:** Better maneuverability on narrow paths and in tight spaces. Less stable when parked — it tips sideways easily on uneven ground.

**Dual wheels:** Dramatically more stable on slopes and soft ground. Harder to tip, but wider — they may not fit through standard garden gates (check the width). Better for heavy loads and uneven terrain.

### Frame and Tray Materials

**Steel trays** (10–14 gauge) are the most durable. They handle rocks, concrete, and rough treatment. They rust over time if stored outdoors, but a coat of paint or a cover adds years. Poly trays never rust and are lighter, but crack under extreme loads or in freezing temperatures. For heavy garden use (soil, stone, mulch), 14-gauge steel or thick polyethylene (1/4-inch+) is the minimum.

**Handles:** Wood handles are traditional and comfortable — they absorb vibration naturally. But they splinter, crack, and rot eventually. Steel handles are more durable and typically powder-coated, but they transmit more vibration and get hot in summer sun. Welded steel frames with ergonomic plastic grips are ideal for heavy use.

### Capacity

Wheelbarrows are measured in cubic feet. A standard contractor barrow is 6 cu ft. A garden barrow is typically 4–5 cu ft. A recreational cart may be 2–3 cu ft. Translation: a 6 cu ft load of wet soil weighs approximately 600–700 lbs. Before buying, check the weight rating — not just the volume rating. Most standard wheelbarrows are rated for 300–500 lbs. Heavy-duty models handle 500–800 lbs.

### Dump Angle

The steeper the tray dumps, the easier it is to empty sticky loads like wet soil or concrete. Traditional barrows dump at 35–45 degrees. Better designs use a pivoting frame that dumps to 60+ degrees. For garden use where you'll be hauling and dumping compost, soil, and mulch frequently, a steep dump angle saves real effort.

## FAQ

### Single-wheel vs. dual-wheel wheelbarrow — which should I get?

Dual-wheel is better on sloped, soft, or uneven ground — it won't tip when you stop to take a break. Single-wheel is better through narrow gates, on garden paths, and for dumping accurately (one wheel pivots more precisely). If your garden has significant slopes or you regularly haul heavy loads over soft soil, get dual-wheel. If you navigate tight spaces or store your barrow in a narrow shed, get single-wheel. For most suburban gardeners with moderate hills and standard path widths, single-wheel is sufficient and more maneuverable.

### How much weight can a typical garden wheelbarrow handle?

A standard 6 cu ft contractor wheelbarrow handles 600–800 lbs of wet concrete or soil. A 4–5 cu ft garden barrow handles 300–500 lbs. The limiting factor is usually not the tray or frame — it's the tire and your ability to push the load. Above 300 lbs, pneumatic tire pressure matters significantly. Above 500 lbs, the person pushing the barrow needs to be reasonably fit. For most homeowners, loads over 400 lbs are rare outside of bulk soil deliveries and gravel projects.

### Flat-free vs. pneumatic tires — which is better for garden use?

Pneumatic tires ride more smoothly and are a meaningful comfort improvement on rough ground and over long distances. Flat-free tires eliminate the most common failure point of wheelbarrows — flats from thorns, nails, and sidewall punctures. If your garden tools live in a shed and your yard doesn't have puncture hazards (thorny weeds, construction debris, gravel), pneumatic is more comfortable. If you work on rough ground, near construction, or want zero maintenance, go flat-free. The Marathon 54505's flat-free tires are the right choice for most homeowners.

### Can I leave my wheelbarrow outside?

A poly tray wheelbarrow with a steel frame (Ames, Marathon) can be left outside with a cover — the tray won't rust, but the frame will eventually. A steel tray barrow (True Temper) left outdoors uncovered will rust within a year. At minimum, keep it off the ground (on a paving stone or scrap wood) and covered with a tarp. Under a porch or overhang is better. A wheelbarrow stored indoors in a shed or garage will last 3–5 times longer than one left out in the elements.

### How do I maintain a wheelbarrow?

Three things will kill a wheelbarrow: rust, flat tires, and seized wheel bearings. To prevent rust, store it dry and touch up paint chips on steel trays immediately. To prevent flats, keep pneumatic tires at the rated PSI (typically 20–40 PSI) and avoid running over sharp debris. To prevent seized bearings, apply a few drops of grease or machine oil to the wheel bearings every spring. That's it — three minutes of maintenance per year keeps a wheelbarrow running for decades. The rest is structural — if the tray cracks or the frame bends, it's time to replace.
