A good pair of garden gloves should protect you from thorns, keep your hands dry, and last more than one season. But most of them won't.

## What to Look For in Garden Gloves

### Material

The glove material determines everything about comfort, protection, and lifespan.

**Leather** (cowhide, goatskin, deerskin) is the gold standard for durability. Goatskin is the best balance of dexterity and toughness — thinner than cowhide but more abrasion-resistant. Deerskin is the softest and most flexible but wears faster. Cowhide is the most durable but stiffest and least comfortable in hot weather. Leather gloves mold to your hand over time and last years with occasional conditioning.

**Nitrile coating** (dipped cotton or nylon gloves) provides excellent grip and waterproof protection at a good price. The coating wears off over time, but they're cheap enough to replace seasonally. Nitrile gloves are the best choice for wet work, mulching, and handling chemicals.

**Cotton** woven gloves are comfortable and breathable but offer almost no protection beyond keeping dirt off your hands. They're fine for light tasks like planting seedlings or potting. They fall apart fast.

**Synthetic materials** like nylon, spandex, and polyurethane are used for lightweight, form-fitting gloves. They're the best for dexterity (picking seeds, handling small pots) but offer minimal protection from thorns or abrasion.

### Fit

Garden gloves that don't fit are dangerous. Too-loose gloves cause blisters from the fabric rubbing, and you lose grip on tools. Too-tight gloves restrict blood flow and tire your hands faster. Most brands use S-XXL sizing based on palm circumference and middle finger length. Measure your dominant hand at the widest part of the palm (excluding the thumb) and check the brand's sizing chart. A good fit is snug but not tight — you should be able to make a full fist without the glove pulling.

### Length

Standard garden gloves come to the wrist or an inch above. Long-cuff gloves (gauntlets or elbow-length) protect your forearms from scratches, thorns, and sun exposure. If you work with thorny plants (roses, blackberries, barberry), get gauntlet-style gloves that extend at least 6 inches past the wrist.

### Grip

The palm and finger grip pattern is critical for handling wet tools, slimy weeds, and trowels with mud-covered handles. Nitrile dots, rubber palm coatings, and textured leather all provide good grip. Smooth leather and plain cotton have poor grip when wet. For wet work, look for nitrile-dipped or textured latex palms.

### Touchscreen Compatibility

Not all "touchscreen gloves" are the same. Most use conductive thread woven into the thumb and index fingertips. Some work well, some barely work at all. If you regularly check your phone for plant IDs, garden apps, or photos, test the glove before committing — bring your phone to the store.

## FAQ

### How often should I replace garden gloves?

Leather gloves: every 1-3 years, depending on frequency of use and care. Condition them twice per season to extend lifespan. Nitrile-coated gloves: every 2-4 months with regular use. The coating wears off gradually — replace when you start losing grip. Cotton/bamboo gloves: every 1-2 months. They're essentially consumables.

### Can I machine wash garden gloves?

Leather: no. Wipe clean and condition. Some synthetic leather blends (FELCO) can be machine washed on gentle. Nitrile-coated gloves: yes, gentle cycle, air dry. Cotton and bamboo: yes, machine washable. Always check the manufacturer's instructions — some gloves have specific care requirements.

### What gloves work for both thorns and wet conditions?

No single glove does both well. For thorny work in wet conditions, buy two pairs: the West County Gardener leather for thorn protection and the Showa 282 for waterproof work. Use each when the conditions match. Leather that gets wet repeatedly will stiffen and crack.

### Should I buy a single expensive pair or multiple cheap pairs?

Two pairs — one good pair for heavy work (Briers or FELCO) and a pack of cheap touchscreen gloves for light tasks (Handlandy or Pine Tree Tools). The expensive pair handles pruning, weeding, and thorn clearing. The cheap pair handles potting, planting, and quick tasks where you'd rather not hunt for your good gloves.

### How do I make garden gloves last longer?

Dry them after every use — don't leave them wet in the garage or potting shed. For leather gloves, apply a leather conditioner or mink oil twice per season. For nitrile gloves, avoid leaving them in direct sun (UV degrades the coating). Have multiple pairs so you're not wearing the same pair every day — rotating extends the life of each pair significantly.
