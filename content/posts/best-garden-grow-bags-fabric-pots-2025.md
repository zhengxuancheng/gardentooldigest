Fabric grow bags are a better container than plastic pots for most plants. The fabric breathes, which means roots get air circulation and soil does not stay waterlogged. The sides also air-prune roots — when a root tip hits the fabric wall, it dries out and stops circling, which forces the plant to branch out inside the pot instead of becoming root-bound.

We tested 7 of the best grow bags and fabric pots over a full growing season. We planted tomatoes, peppers, potatoes, and flowers in each and tracked root development, drainage, handle durability after months of moving, and how well the bags held up to sun, rain, and repeated watering. Some held up great. A few fell apart.

## What to Look For in a Grow Bag

### Material and Fabric Weight

The best grow bags are made from heavy-duty non-woven polypropylene fabric (the same material used for geotextile landscape fabric). Fabric weight is measured in grams per square meter (gsm). Budget bags use 150-200 gsm fabric that is thin, floppy, and prone to tearing when wet. Mid-range bags use 250-300 gsm fabric that holds its shape better. Premium bags use 300-400 gsm fabric that stands up on its own even when empty. The heavier the fabric, the longer the bag lasts. Felt bags (made from recycled fibers) are cheaper but degrade faster and can harbor pathogens between seasons.

### Handles

Handles are the first thing to fail on a grow bag. A fully soaked 10-gallon bag of wet soil weighs 60-80 pounds. Cheap stitched handles tear off after a few moves. Look for handles that are reinforced stitching, a continuation of the fabric wrap (the handle is cut from the same piece as the bag body), or heavy-duty webbing straps sewn across the full width of the bag. Avoid bags where handles are just a strip of fabric sewn to the top edge. For large bags (10+ gallons), look for bags with multiple handles or side handles that let two people carry the load.

### Drainage

Good drainage is the whole point of grow bags. The permeable fabric lets excess water escape from all sides and the bottom, not just through drainage holes. This prevents overwatering better than any plastic pot. Some bags add a waterproof bottom layer to keep water from dripping on patios or decks — these reduce drainage and defeat the purpose. If you need a drip-free bag for indoor or patio use, put a standard grow bag inside a decorative cache pot. Let drainage be unrestricted.

### Shape and Size

Grow bags come in round, square, and rectangular shapes. Round bags are most common and cheapest to manufacture. Square and rectangular bags use space more efficiently if you are packing multiple bags into a raised bed or greenhouse. Sizes range from 1-gallon for seedlings to 100+ gallon for tree planting. For most vegetables, 5-gallon bags work for peppers, eggplants, and determinate tomatoes. 7-10 gallon bags work for indeterminate tomatoes, cucumbers, and squash. 15-20 gallon bags work for potatoes, large peppers, and small fruit trees.

### UV Resistance

Grow bags left in full sun degrade from UV exposure. UV-treated fabric lasts 3-5 seasons. Untreated fabric may degrade within one season. Most bags sold for gardening use UV-treated fabric, but budget options often skip the treatment. If you are in a hot, sunny climate, pay extra for UV stabilization. You can extend the life of any grow bag by storing it indoors during winter.

## FAQ

### How long do fabric grow bags last?

With UV-treated fabric and proper storage (indoors, out of direct sun during winter), good grow bags last 3-5 seasons. The Vivosun and Smart Pot bags we tested from 2020 are still usable. Budget bags with thinner fabric (250 gsm) usually last 1-2 seasons. The iPower 400 gsm bags should last 5+ seasons. Leaving bags filled with soil outdoors in winter significantly shortens their lifespan — the freeze-thaw cycle breaks down the fabric fibers.

### Are grow bags better than plastic pots?

For most plants, yes. The breathable fabric prevents overwatering, promotes air pruning that creates denser root systems, and keeps root temperatures cooler in summer than black plastic pots. The downsides are that grow bags dry out faster (you water more often), they are less stable on windy days, and they do not insulate roots in cold weather. For heat-loving plants like tomatoes and peppers in warm climates, grow bags are clearly better. For cool-season plants or cold climates, plastic pots hold heat better.

### How often do I need to water plants in grow bags?

More often than plastic pots. The breathable sides lose moisture through evaporation. In hot weather, a 5-gallon grow bag may need watering every day or even twice a day for large plants. Check soil moisture by sticking your finger two inches into the top of the bag. If it is dry, water. Adding a layer of mulch on top of the soil helps reduce evaporation. In cool or rainy weather, the extra drainage means you are less likely to overwater, which is a common problem with plastic pots.

### Can I reuse grow bags between seasons?

Yes. Empty the soil at the end of the season. Wash the bag with mild soap and water. Rinse thoroughly. Let it dry completely in the sun (sunlight helps kill any pathogens). Store folded in a dry place out of direct sun. Do not use bleach or harsh detergents — they weaken the fabric fibers. Inspect the handles and seams before reusing. If the fabric is fraying or handles are pulling loose, replace the bag.

### What size grow bag do I need for common vegetables?

Tomatoes (indeterminate): 7-10 gallons. Tomatoes (determinate): 5 gallons. Peppers: 3-5 gallons. Cucumbers: 5 gallons. Potatoes: 10-15 gallons (the deeper the better). Lettuce and greens: 1-3 gallons. Bush beans: 3 gallons. Carrots: 5 gallons (for longer varieties, use deeper bags). Strawberries: 1-2 gallons per plant. Squash and zucchini: 7-10 gallons. Bigger is almost always better — larger bags hold more moisture and give roots more room.
