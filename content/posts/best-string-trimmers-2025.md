A lawn mower handles the open grass, but it can't reach fence lines, garden beds, or around trees. That's where a string trimmer — often called a weed eater or weed whacker — earns its keep. It's the detail tool that transforms a "just mowed" lawn into a manicured landscape.

The string trimmer market has evolved rapidly. Today's top cordless models rival gas trimmers in power while eliminating fuel mixing, pull cords, and exhaust fumes. But with options ranging from budget plug-in models to professional-grade battery systems, picking the right one means weighing cutting width, battery compatibility, line feed mechanism, weight, and price.

## Why Go Cordless? (And When Gas Still Makes Sense)

### The Battery Revolution

Cordless string trimmers have closed the gap with gas. A top-tier 56V or 80V battery trimmer delivers enough torque to slice through thick weeds and tall grass without bogging down — and starts with a button press instead of a pull cord. No fuel to mix, no carburetor to clean, no spark plug to replace.

### Quieter Operation

Gas trimmers run at 90–100 decibels — hearing protection is mandatory. Most battery trimmers operate at 65–75 dB, about the level of a vacuum cleaner. You can trim early on a Saturday without waking the neighborhood.

### Zero Maintenance, Zero Emissions

No oil changes. No fuel stabilizer. No gummed-up carburetor from ethanol-blended gas sitting in the tank all winter. Battery trimmers produce zero direct emissions and require essentially no maintenance beyond occasionally cleaning the guard and replacing the line.

### When Gas Still Wins

If you're clearing acres of overgrown brush or running a commercial landscaping business, a gas trimmer still offers unlimited runtime (just add fuel) and marginally more raw power. For the other 95% of us, battery has caught up — and the convenience gap only widens every year.

## Understanding String Trimmer Line

### Line Diameter: Thicker Isn't Always Better

String trimmer line typically runs from 0.065 to 0.105 inches in diameter. Here's what to know:

- **0.065–0.080 inch:** Light duty — grass, clover, soft weeds. Maximum compatibility with most trimmers.
- **0.080–0.095 inch:** Medium duty — thick grass, dandelions, light brush. The sweet spot for homeowners.
- **0.095–0.105 inch:** Heavy duty — woody weeds, overgrown areas. Requires a powerful trimmer to spin effectively without bogging.

### Line Shape Matters

- **Round line:** Most common, most durable, least likely to break. Good all-purpose choice.
- **Twisted line:** Cuts more aggressively, slightly quieter in operation. Wears faster than round.
- **Square or star-shaped line:** Sharp edges cut cleanly through thick stalks. Wears the fastest but delivers the cleanest cut.

### Bump Feed vs. Automatic Feed

**Bump feed:** Tap the head on the ground to advance a measured amount of line. Most common, reliable once you get the rhythm down. All our top picks use bump feed.  

**Automatic feed:** Deploys new line when the trimmer senses it's too short. Convenient but can waste line if the mechanism advances too aggressively.

## FAQ

### What cutting width do I need for my yard?

For yards under 1/4 acre, a 12–15 inch cutting width is perfectly adequate. For 1/4 to 1/2 acre, go with 15–16 inches. Over 1/2 acre, a 16–17 inch width with a powerful motor saves real time. Wider isn't always better — it adds weight and makes navigating tight spaces around flower beds and trees more difficult.

### Are battery string trimmers as powerful as gas?

Top-tier battery trimmers like the EGO ST1623T and Greenworks 80V deliver power that matches mid-range gas models. They'll handle thick grass, weeds, and light brush without issue. Gas still has an edge for commercial-scale work or clearing acres of heavy overgrowth, but for residential use, battery has effectively caught up — and you don't have to deal with fuel mixing or pull cords.

### What's the difference between a straight shaft and curved shaft trimmer?

**Straight shaft:** Longer reach, better for edging along sidewalks and driveways, generally transmits power more efficiently. Preferred by professionals and anyone over 5'8". All our top picks are straight shaft.  

**Curved shaft:** Lighter, more maneuverable, better for tight spaces and shorter users. Typically found on budget and light-duty models. Less effective for edging.

### How often do I need to replace trimmer line?

With moderate use (about 30 minutes per week), a spool of line lasts 4–8 weeks. Heavy users, rocky soil conditions, and frequent trimming along concrete edges all accelerate line wear. Keep a spare spool of 0.080-inch round line on hand — it's inexpensive ($8–$15) and eliminates the frustration of running out mid-job.

### Can I use a string trimmer for edging along sidewalks and driveways?

Yes — most straight-shaft trimmers can be tilted 90 degrees to edge along hard surfaces. Some models (like the WORX WG163) have dedicated edging modes with guide wheels for cleaner, straighter results. For professional-looking edges, a dedicated walk-behind edger produces sharper lines, but a trimmer held at an angle does the job for the vast majority of homeowners.
