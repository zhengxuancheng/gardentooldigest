A fire pit turns a backyard into a gathering spot. Guests gravitate to it. Conversations happen around it. Marshmallows get roasted. But the difference between a good fire pit and a frustrating one comes down to how easy it is to start, how much smoke it produces, where you sit relative to the heat, and what you have to clean up the next morning.

We tested 6 fire pits across different backyard sizes and use cases. Wood-burning, propane, smokeless, and portable — each one has a situation where it makes sense and situations where it does not.

## What to Look For in an Outdoor Fire Pit

### Fuel Type: Wood vs. Propane vs. Natural Gas

Wood-burning fire pits are the traditional choice. Wood is cheap or free if you have a supply, the fire is authentic, and you get the full campfire experience. The downsides are smoke, ash cleanup, sparks, and the time it takes to build and maintain a fire.

Propane fire pits start instantly with a push-button ignition, produce no smoke, and leave no ash. You control the flame height with a dial. The trade-off is fuel cost (a 20-pound propane tank lasts 8-12 hours at medium flame) and the missing "campfire" feel — no crackle, no smell of woodsmoke.

Natural gas fire pits connect to your home gas line. They share the convenience of propane with the benefit of never running out of fuel. Installation requires a gas line connection and usually a professional, raising the upfront cost significantly.

### Smokeless Technology

Double-wall fire pits (like Solo Stove and Tiki) use secondary combustion: air is drawn from below, heated between the walls, and injected through holes near the top of the fire pit. This burns the smoke particles that would otherwise drift into your face. The result is 80-90% less smoke than a traditional open fire pit. The trade-off is that smokeless pits burn through wood faster because they are more efficient — a bundle of firewood that lasts 2 hours in a standard pit lasts 1 to 1.5 hours in a smokeless design.

### Heat Output and Direction

Open fire pits radiate heat in all directions — you feel warm from any angle but only within a certain radius. Propane fire pits often direct heat upward or have a reflective bowl that sends heat outward. Smokeless pits concentrate heat upward due to the airflow design, which means you need to stand closer to feel the warmth. A fire pit is only as useful as the distance you can comfortably sit from it on a cool night.

### Size and Portability

A 36-inch fire pit dominates a patio but does not move. A 15-inch portable pit fits in a car trunk and goes to the beach or campsite. Your available storage space and typical use case dictate the size. Most first-time buyers underestimate how large a fire pit is — a 30-inch bowl takes up a 4-foot diameter circle when you include the safety clearance around it.

### Materials and Durability

Steel fire pits rust within 2-3 seasons without a protective coating or weather cover. Stainless steel resists rust indefinitely but costs more. Cast iron holds heat longest but is heavy and cracks if stored wet. Corten (weathering) steel forms a rust patina that protects the metal underneath — it looks rusty but stays structurally sound for decades. Most mid-range fire pits use painted carbon steel, which needs a cover and occasional touch-up paint.

## FAQ

### Are smokeless fire pits really smokeless?

The short answer is "mostly." Double-wall fire pits like Solo Stove and Breeo produce 80-90% less smoke than an open fire pit, but they still produce some smoke during the first 5-10 minutes while the fire establishes itself. Once the secondary combustion kicks in, visible smoke drops to near zero. Wind can disrupt the airflow and increase smoke. Wet or green wood produces significant smoke regardless of the fire pit design. Use dry, seasoned wood with less than 20% moisture content for the best results.

### Is propane or wood better for a fire pit?

Propane is better for convenience, cleanliness, and control. Wood is better for atmosphere, heat output, and cost. If you want to sit outside on a Tuesday evening after dinner and have a fire for an hour, propane wins. If you want a 4-hour backyard gathering with friends that feels like a campfire, wood wins. Many people start with a propane pit for the convenience and buy a wood pit later for the weekends.

### How far should a fire pit be from a house?

Minimum 10 feet from any structure, including your house, fence, shed, and deck railing. Check your local fire codes — some municipalities require 15-25 feet. The fire pit should also sit on a non-combustible surface (stone, brick, concrete, gravel). Do not place a fire pit directly on wood decking, grass, or dry leaves. A spark screen is recommended for wood-burning pits at any distance.

### What is the best way to put out a fire pit?

For wood fires: let the fire burn down to ash, then spread the embers with a poker and let them cool. Pour water slowly over the ashes while stirring — avoid dumping a bucket of water that creates a steam explosion and ash cloud. For propane fires: turn the gas valve off at the source (the tank, not the burner dial). Wait 30 seconds, then turn the burner dial to off to clear the line. Never leave a fire pit unattended while it is burning.

### How do I clean a fire pit?

For wood pits: remove cold ash and dispose of it. Ash is alkaline and good for garden soil in small amounts. Wipe the interior with a dry cloth. Do not use water inside a steel bowl — moisture accelerates rust. Cover the pit when not in use. For propane pits: the lava rock or fire glass lasts indefinitely — rinse it with a hose once a year to remove dust and spider webs. Wipe the burner cover dry before lighting.

### Can I cook on a fire pit?

Yes, but only on fire pits designed for cooking or with a cooking grate accessory. The Solo Stove Bonfire and Breeo X Series have cooking grates. The Tiki Brand 28-inch includes a cooking grate. Never cook directly over a propane fire pit's burner — the lava rock can contain mineral fibers that are not food-safe. Use a dedicated grill grate that sits above the lava rock level.
