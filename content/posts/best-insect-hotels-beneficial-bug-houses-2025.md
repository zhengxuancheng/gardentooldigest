I bought my first insect hotel thinking it would look cute in the garden and maybe a couple of bees would check it out. I didn't expect it to become the most active corner of the yard. Mason bees moved into the tubes. Lacewings set up shop in the pine cone section. A family of earwigs claimed the bamboo hollows. The thing was buzzing within a month.

Not all insect hotels work that well. I tested 7 models over two growing seasons, and the results were uneven. Some attracted beneficial bugs immediately. A couple stayed empty. One fell apart in the rain after six weeks.

## What to Look For in a Garden Insect Hotel

### Material Quality

The best insect hotels are made from untreated solid wood with natural filling materials. Treated or painted wood can offgas chemicals that repel the insects you're trying to attract. Composite materials and plastics don't breathe properly and trap moisture, which leads to mold growth inside the tubes.

Solid wood should be at least 1 cm thick. Thin plywood warps and delaminates within one season. Cedar is the best choice for longevity. Pine works if it's untreated but rots faster. Bamboo is excellent for the nesting tubes but shouldn't be the main structural material.

### Tube and Cavity Design

Different insects need different cavity sizes. Solitary bees prefer tubes that are 2-10 mm in diameter and 10-15 cm deep. The tubes should be closed at one end — bees won't nest in open-ended tubes because predators can get in from both sides. Lacewings like narrow slits and crevices. Ladybugs prefer larger chambers where they can cluster for winter hibernation.

The tubes should be removable for cleaning. Fixed tubes that can't be opened or replaced become breeding grounds for mites and fungal diseases over time. The best designs have replaceable bamboo inserts or paper tubes.

### Roof and Drainage

An insect hotel without a proper roof isn't an insect hotel — it's a mold farm. Rain gets into the cavities and the insects drown or the eggs rot. A wide overhang, sloped roof, or both is essential. The roof should extend at least 2-3 inches beyond the sides and front of the house.

Some models add a drainage gap between the back of the house and the mounting surface. This is a smart feature because it prevents moisture from wicking into the structure from the wall.

### Facing Direction

This sounds like a minor detail but it matters: insect hotels should face south or southeast to catch morning sun. Solitary bees are cold-blooded and need morning sunlight to warm up before they start foraging. An insect hotel facing north will get limited use. The best models come with mounting instructions that explain this.

### Maintenance Access

Insect hotels need annual cleaning to prevent disease buildup. The best designs have a removable back panel or individual tubes that slide out for replacement. Ones that are fully sealed or glued together can't be cleaned and should be considered seasonal decorations rather than functional habitats.

## FAQ

### Where should I put my insect hotel?

Mount it on a wall, fence, or post that faces south or southeast. It should get morning sun to warm the insects up for the day. Place it 3-6 feet off the ground and near flowering plants so the bees have a food source within a short flight. Avoid sheltered corners where the house won't get any sun, and definitely avoid full shade.

### Do insect hotels actually work, or are they just garden decor?

Good ones work. Bad ones don't. The difference is whether the design accounts for real insect needs: tubes closed at one end, proper roof overhang, natural untreated materials, appropriate tube diameters. A well-designed insect hotel will attract mason bees, leafcutter bees, lacewings, ladybugs, earwigs, and other beneficial insects. A poorly designed one will sit empty or, worse, become a breeding ground for mites and parasites.

### How often do I need to clean an insect hotel?

Once a year, in late winter or early spring before the insects emerge. Remove old nesting materials and replace with fresh ones. If you notice mites, mold, or parasites in a tube, remove it immediately. A clean hotel attracts more insects than one with accumulated debris. Some insects (like ladybugs) use hotels for winter hibernation, so wait until they've left in spring before cleaning.

### What bugs will move into my insect hotel?

Solitary bees (mason bees and leafcutter bees) are the main target. They'll nest in the tubes. Lacewings prefer the pine cone or straw sections. Ladybugs will use the larger chambers for winter shelter. Earwigs are often seen as pests but they eat aphids, so they're actually beneficial. Small spiders, beetles, and hoverflies may also take up residence. You probably won't get honeybees or bumblebees — they nest in larger cavities like hollow trees or underground burrows.

### Should I buy or build an insect hotel?

Buying is easier and the results are more predictable if you pick a well-designed model. Building is cheaper and lets you customize the materials and dimensions to what insects are in your area. If you build, use untreated cedar or pine, make sure tubes are closed at one end, add a generous roof overhang, and use a mix of tube sizes from 2-10 mm. The Woodlink Cedar house at its price point is hard to beat for cost, and the Nestera is the gold standard if you're willing to spend.
