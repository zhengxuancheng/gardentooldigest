A hammock seems simple until you try to buy one. There are rope hammocks, quilted fabric hammocks, camping hammocks, double hammocks, hammock chairs, and hammocks with spreader bars. And that is before you figure out how to hang the thing. Trees need to be the right distance apart. Posts need to be the right height. A bad setup means you either hit the ground or feel like you are in a slackline competition.

We tested 14 hammocks and 8 stands over a month of outdoor use. Some setups work perfectly from the first hang. Others need modifications, extra hardware, or just more space than most people have.

## What to Look For in a Hammock

**Material and breathability.** Cotton rope hammocks are soft and breathable but rot in rain. Quilted polyester or parachute nylon dries faster and resists mildew. For outdoor use where the hammock stays outside, synthetic fabrics last longer.

**Size.** A single hammock is 48-52 inches wide. A double is 54-60 inches. Most people find a double more comfortable for solo lounging and necessary for two people. Length matters too — a hammock needs about 13-15 feet of hang space.

**Weight capacity.** Most double hammocks support 400-500 pounds. Singles support 250-350 pounds. Check the spec rather than assuming.

**Hardware and suspension.** Rope hammocks come with spreader bars made of wood or metal. Fabric hammocks use carabiners and tree straps. Good hardware uses stainless steel, not painted steel that rusts.

**Stand compatibility.** If you do not have suitable trees, the hammock must work with a stand. Not every hammock fits every stand — check the assembled length.

## FAQ

### Do I need trees or can I use a stand?

You can use either. Trees need to be 12-14 feet apart with trunks at least 6 inches in diameter. If your yard does not have suitable trees, a stand solves the problem. Stands take up about 10-12 feet of ground space and work on grass, patio, or deck surfaces.

### Which material lasts longer outside?

Synthetic fabrics (nylon, polyester) last longer than cotton in outdoor conditions. Cotton rope absorbs water, rots, and fades in direct sunlight. If the hammock stays outside uncovered, go with parachute nylon or polyester webbing. If you bring it inside at night, cotton is more comfortable.

### Can I sleep in a hammock overnight?

Yes, with the right technique. Lay diagonally so your body forms a flat line rather than a banana curve. A camping hammock with a bug net and rain fly makes overnight sleeping more practical. Rope hammocks with spreader bars are not stable enough for sleeping — you can roll out.

### What height should I hang a hammock?

The ideal sit height is about 18 inches off the ground when you are in the hammock. That usually means hanging the hammock 36-48 inches above the ground at the attachment points, depending on the length of the hammock and the suspension hardware. The sag angle should be about 30 degrees from the horizontal.

### How do I keep the hammock from flipping over?

Spreader-bar hammocks are stable and hard to flip. Gathered-end hammocks (no spreader bars) can flip if you lean too far over one side. The risk is low for sitting and lounging. For sleeping, use a hammock with a structural ridgeline that maintains consistent sag.
