Throwing fertilizer by hand is a gamble. You'll get dark green stripes where your hand was heavy, pale patches where it was light, and the sinking feeling you've wasted half the bag. A good spreader is the difference between a patchy lawn and one that glows with uniform health. It pays for itself by eliminating wasted product.

Fertilizer spreaders come in three types: broadcast spreaders that fling granules in a wide arc, drop spreaders that release material in a precise strip directly beneath the hopper, and handheld models for small lawns. We tested 6 of the best in 2025 to help you decide.

## How We Evaluated

We put each spreader through multiple lawn applications and scored them on:

- **Spread uniformity** — is coverage even, or are there gaps?
- **Capacity** — how much product the hopper holds per fill
- **Spread width & edge control** — coverage speed vs. precision around flower beds
- **Build quality** — plastic vs. metal gears, pneumatic vs. plastic tires
- **Ease of calibration** — can you set the application rate accurately?

## Broadcast vs. Drop vs. Handheld: Which Type?

**Broadcast (rotary) spreaders** use a spinning impeller to fling granules in a wide semicircle — typically 5 to 12 feet. They're the fastest option for medium and large lawns, handling fertilizer, grass seed, and ice melt. The downside: less precision around edges, and wind can carry light granules beyond your target.

**Drop spreaders** release material straight down through holes spanning the hopper width (18–24 inches). Zero drift onto sidewalks or flower beds. The downside: they're slow — each pass covers only the hopper width, and wheel-to-wheel alignment must be precise to avoid missed strips or double-dosed lines.

**Handheld spreaders** — battery-powered or manual crank — are the lightweight choice for lawns under 2,500 square feet and spot treatments. Cheap, compact, and not practical for anything larger.

## Understanding Spreader Calibration

Calibration is the difference between a green lawn and a burned one. Most fertilizer bags include a spreader-setting chart for major brands. If your spreader is listed, use that setting. If not, start at setting 3–4 (out of 10) for most broadcast spreaders and adjust.

For precision, do a test run: fill the spreader with a known weight of fertilizer, spread over a measured area, and weigh the remainder. Match your output to the product's recommended rate. Walk at a consistent, brisk pace — 3 to 3.5 mph is standard. Slowing down increases application rate; speeding up decreases it.

## FAQ

### What's the difference between a broadcast and drop spreader?

Broadcast spreaders fling granules in a wide arc using a spinning disc — fast but less precise around edges. Drop spreaders release material straight down with zero drift — precise but slow, requiring perfect wheel-to-wheel alignment. For most homeowners, broadcast is the better all-around choice.

### How do I avoid dark-and-light stripes in my lawn?

Striping happens when passes don't overlap correctly. Broadcast spreaders throw heaviest near the spreader and taper at the edges. Overlap each pass by 20–30% of the spread width. If your spreader throws 8 feet, walk your next pass about 5–6 feet from the previous one.

### Can I use the same spreader for fertilizer, grass seed, and ice melt?

Yes, but clean thoroughly between uses. Fertilizer and ice melt are corrosive; rinse the hopper and impeller with water after each application and dry completely. Consider a dedicated cheap spreader for ice melt in snowy climates.

### Are pneumatic tires worth it on a push spreader?

If your lawn is smooth and flat, plastic wheels are fine. For bumpy or uneven terrain, pneumatic tires absorb bumps rather than bouncing the hopper — producing a more consistent spread pattern and less fatigue. You'll need to check tire pressure periodically.

### How do I store my spreader?

Empty remaining fertilizer after every use. Rinse the hopper and impeller with a garden hose (avoid spraying the gearbox). Dry completely. Wipe metal parts with WD-40 or silicone spray to prevent rust. Store indoors or under cover — fertilizer residue attracts moisture.
