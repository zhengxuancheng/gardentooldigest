There is a moment in every gardener's spring when they walk outside and discover something ate the lettuce, dug up the bulbs, or trampled the tomatoes. Rabbits, deer, groundhogs, neighborhood dogs, and even your own chickens can destroy weeks of work in one night. The solution is not always an eight-foot privacy fence. Most gardens just need targeted protection in the right places.

We tested seven fencing and protection products across four categories: deer fencing, rabbit barriers, border edging, and bird netting. Some keep animals out. Others keep your plants in. Pick the wrong one and you will know within a week.

## What to Look for in Garden Fencing and Protection

### Height Requirements by Animal

Different animals need different fence heights. Deer need at least 7 to 8 feet, since they jump. Rabbits need 2 feet, with the bottom buried or staked to the ground. Groundhogs burrow, so the fence needs to extend 6 to 12 inches below the surface. Birds only need exclusion netting draped directly over the plants.

### Material and Durability

Galvanized steel mesh lasts decades. Polypropylene netting lasts one to three seasons depending on UV exposure. Plastic border edging can crack in freeze-thaw cycles if it is cheap polyethylene instead of recycled rubber or polypropylene. Match the material to how permanent you want the installation to be.

### Aesthetic Impact

A garden fence defines the space visually. Black coated mesh disappears against foliage better than bare galvanized wire. Decorative border edging in wood-look or stone-look finishes makes the garden look intentional. Function matters more than looks, but you have to look at it every day.

## FAQ

### What is the cheapest way to keep deer out of a garden?

Seven-foot polypropylene mesh on metal T-posts. A 100-foot roll costs around 30 dollars. T-posts add about 3 dollars each. Total for a small garden runs 80 to 120 dollars. The YARDGARD kit is the easiest starting point if you are not sure about sizing.

### How do I stop rabbits from digging under a fence?

Extend the fence 6 inches below ground, bent outward in an L-shape. Rabbits dig at the base. If they hit buried wire, they move on. You can also use landscape staples to pin the bottom edge flat against the soil surface.

### Will garden fencing keep my dog out of the vegetable beds?

A 3 to 4 foot fence will stop most dogs. Jumpers and climbers need a taller fence or a top extension angled inward. The Greenes lattice fence works well for maintaining visibility while keeping dogs out.

### How long does polypropylene garden netting last before UV damage makes it brittle?

Two to three years in full sun exposure. Less in high UV climates. The VIVOSUN netting starts showing brittleness around the start of the third season. Replace it proactively rather than discovering holes mid-season.

### Can I use border edging to contain invasive plants?

Standard 6-inch edging slows them down but does not stop them. Mint, bamboo, and running grasses spread through or under shallow edging. You need a rigid barrier buried 18 to 24 inches deep for true containment.

## The Bottom Line

Start with the problem you actually have, not the fence you think you need. Rabbits in the vegetable bed get the Dalen rabbit fence with a buried L-flange at the base. Deer hitting the hostas get the YARDGARD deer fence. Birds stripping the blueberries get the VIVOSUN netting on a hoop frame.
