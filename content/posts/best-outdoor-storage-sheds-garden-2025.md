Garden tools have a way of accumulating. You start with a shovel and some pruners. A few years later there is a leaf blower, a string trimmer, a pressure washer, a wheelbarrow, bags of fertilizer, pots, stakes, and a hose that kinks the second you look away. A shed keeps all of it organized and dry instead of rusting in a pile under a tarp in the garage corner.

We tested 7 outdoor storage sheds — small resin boxes for hand tools, medium plastic sheds for mowers and trimmers, large steel buildings for full workshop setups, and wooden structures for those who want something that looks like it belongs in a garden. Each was evaluated on ease of assembly, weather resistance over six months, usable interior volume versus claimed size, and whether a typical riding mower actually fits through the door.

## What to Look For in a Garden Storage Shed

Here are the factors that separate a shed that lasts from one that blows over in a storm.

### Material: Resin vs. Steel vs. Wood

Resin (polyethylene or polypropylene) sheds are the most popular choice for homeowners. They are lightweight, do not rust, never need painting, and assembly is manageable with two people. They are the least expensive option per cubic foot of storage. Resin sheds fade and become brittle over 5-7 years in direct sunlight.

Steel sheds are stronger per pound than any other material, fire-resistant, and rodent-proof. They rust if the galvanized coating is scratched. They require a level foundation more than any other type, because steel panels do not flex to accommodate uneven ground. Condensation inside steel sheds is a real issue in humid climates.

Wooden sheds are the most attractive and most repairable — you can replace a rotted board without replacing the entire wall. They require the most maintenance: staining or sealing every 2-3 years, checking for rot, and anchoring against wind. Wood sheds are heavier and more expensive than comparable resin sheds.

### Foundation Requirements

Every shed needs a level foundation, but the material determines how critical this is. Resin sheds are forgiving — their plastic floors flex slightly over uneven ground. Steel sheds require a perfectly level concrete or paver base; a steel panel that is an eighth-inch out of square will not align with its neighbor. Wood sheds need pressure-treated skids or a concrete slab, and they will rot if placed directly on soil.

Most mid-size sheds (under 8x8 feet) work fine on a gravel base with pressure-treated timber beams. Larger sheds (10x12 feet or bigger) need a concrete slab.

### Door Configuration

The biggest practical difference between shed models is the door. Double doors (barn-style swing doors) provide the widest access — essential for wheelbarrows, mowers, and large equipment. Slide-over doors are convenient but reduce interior wall space. Single-person doors save space but require awkward maneuvering of anything wider than a rake handle. Measure your largest tool before buying. A typical riding mower needs at least a 48-inch-wide door opening.

### Ventilation and Condensation

The leading cause of tool rust inside a shed is condensation, not rain getting in. When warm air hits the cool interior of a metal shed, moisture forms on every surface. Resin and wood sheds breathe better than steel, but all sheds benefit from a small louvered vent near the roofline. Some models include vents; others require an aftermarket kit.

### Wind and Snow Load

Shed manufacturers provide wind and snow load ratings, but they assume proper anchoring. A resin shed on a gravel pad without anchors will lift in a 40 mph gust. Check that the shed comes with anchoring hardware — ground augers for resin sheds, concrete anchor bolts for steel and wood. For areas with heavy snowfall, look for a roof pitch of at least 4:12 to shed snow instead of collecting it.

## FAQ

### Do I need a permit for a garden shed?

Most municipalities require a permit for sheds larger than 120 square feet (10x12 feet). Many allow sheds up to 10x10 or 8x12 without a permit, but setback requirements from property lines still apply. Check with your local building department before buying. Getting the wrong size can result in a forced removal.

### Can I assemble a shed by myself?

Small resin sheds (6x4 and smaller) are doable alone. Medium and large sheds (8x6 and up) require at least two people. Steel and wood sheds need two to three people for panel alignment and roof assembly. Plan for a helper for anything larger than a compact storage box.

### How do I prevent rust inside a metal shed?

Ventilation is the main solution. Add a louvered ridge vent or gable vent to allow air circulation. Keep tools dry before storing them. A thin layer of oil on tool metal surfaces prevents rust from forming. Silica gel containers placed inside the shed absorb moisture. In humid climates, a steel shed may not be the best choice.

### Should I put the shed on a concrete slab or gravel?

Gravel is sufficient for resin sheds up to 8x6 feet — it drains well and is easy to level. Steel sheds over 8x8 feet should sit on a concrete slab to prevent floor flex and panel misalignment. Wood sheds need a concrete slab or well-compacted gravel with pressure-treated skids to prevent ground contact rot.

### What is the best way to organize tools inside a shed?

Wall-mounted pegboards and slatwall panels make the most of vertical space. Floor space should be reserved for large equipment like mowers and wheelbarrows. Install shelves along one wall for pots, chemicals, and supplies. Hang long-handled tools on wall hooks rather than leaning them in a corner — they take up floor space and fall over constantly.
